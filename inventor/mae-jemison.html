Reaching for the Stars: The Mae Jemison Story

Mae Carol Jemison was born on October 17, 1956, in Decatur, Alabama, but grew up on the South Side of Chicago, Illinois, after her family moved when she was three years old. Her father, Charlie Jemison, worked as a maintenance supervisor, while her mother, Dorothy, was an elementary school teacher. From her earliest years, Mae demonstrated the curiosity, determination, and brilliance that would eventually take her beyond Earth's atmosphere.

As a young girl watching the Apollo missions on television, Mae was captivated by space exploration. However, she noticed something troubling: all the astronauts were white men. She asked her parents why there were no women astronauts, particularly no Black women. Her mother's response was simple and powerful: "This is just the way it is now, but it doesn't have to stay that way." This message‚Äîthat the present doesn't determine the future‚Äîbecame the foundation of Mae's approach to life.

Mae's childhood was filled with both scientific curiosity and artistic expression. She loved science, particularly astronomy and biology, spending hours observing the stars and conducting her own experiments. But she also studied dance, becoming accomplished in modern, African, Caribbean, and Japanese dance forms. This combination of scientific rigor and artistic creativity would characterize her entire career, proving that one doesn't have to choose between different passions.

Academic Excellence: From Stanford to Cornell Medical School

Mae Jemison's academic brilliance became evident early. She graduated from Morgan Park High School in Chicago at age 16 and entered Stanford University in 1973 on a National Achievement Scholarship. At Stanford, Mae refused to limit herself to a single field. She pursued a double major, earning a Bachelor of Science in Chemical Engineering while simultaneously earning a Bachelor of Arts in African and African-American Studies. She graduated in 1977, demonstrating that excellence in STEM fields could coexist with deep engagement in cultural and social studies.

During her time at Stanford, Mae was active in student organizations including the Black Student Union, serving as its head. She also continued her dance training and even choreographed and produced a musical production. This holistic approach to education‚Äîcombining rigorous science with arts, culture, and community engagement‚Äîreflected Mae's philosophy that human beings are multidimensional and shouldn't be confined to narrow categories.

After Stanford, Mae entered Cornell University Medical College in New York City. While pursuing her medical degree, she traveled to Cuba to study primary health care delivery and to rural Kenya to provide medical services. These experiences opened her eyes to global health disparities and the ways medicine could serve underserved populations. She earned her Doctor of Medicine degree in 1981, becoming both an engineer and a physician‚Äîdual credentials that would prove invaluable for her future career.

Medical Service: From LA to West Africa

After completing her medical degree, Dr. Jemison worked as a general practitioner in Los Angeles, California. However, her commitment to global health and social justice led her to join the Peace Corps in 1983 as a medical officer. She was assigned to Sierra Leone and Liberia in West Africa, where she served until 1985.

As the Area Peace Corps Medical Officer for Sierra Leone and Liberia, Dr. Jemison was responsible for the health care of Peace Corps volunteers and embassy personnel. She managed the medical needs of Americans working in challenging conditions while also providing care to local populations. She supervised medical staff, performed research on hepatitis B vaccines and rabies, and developed guidelines for public health and safety issues.

Her work in West Africa was demanding and sometimes dangerous. She faced outbreaks of disease, limited medical resources, and difficult working conditions. Yet she thrived, demonstrating the leadership abilities, problem-solving skills, and grace under pressure that would later serve her well as an astronaut. The experience also deepened her commitment to using science and technology to address global challenges and improve lives in underserved communities.

Reaching for Space: Becoming an Astronaut

While working in West Africa, Dr. Jemison never abandoned her childhood dream of becoming an astronaut. When she returned to the United States in 1985, she applied to NASA's astronaut program. The timing was challenging‚Äîjust months after she submitted her application, the Space Shuttle Challenger disaster occurred in January 1986, killing all seven crew members and halting the shuttle program.

NASA stopped accepting new astronaut applications while investigating the disaster and redesigning the shuttle. But when applications reopened in 1987, Mae applied again. This time, she was one of only 15 people selected from a pool of approximately 2,000 applicants. In June 1987, Dr. Mae Jemison officially became a NASA astronaut candidate, beginning a rigorous training program that would prepare her for spaceflight.

The training was intense and comprehensive. Mae studied shuttle systems, learned to operate spacecraft controls, trained in survival techniques, practiced spacewalk procedures in underwater simulations, and prepared her body for the physical demands of space travel. She excelled at all aspects of training, impressing her instructors and fellow astronauts with her technical skills, physical conditioning, and calm demeanor.

September 12, 1992: Making History Above Earth

On September 12, 1992, at 10:23 AM Eastern Time, Space Shuttle Endeavour lifted off from Kennedy Space Center in Florida carrying a crew of seven, including Mission Specialist Dr. Mae Jemison. As the shuttle ascended through Earth's atmosphere and reached orbit, Mae became the first African American woman to travel to space, fulfilling the dream she had held since childhood.

The STS-47 mission, also known as Spacelab-J, was a joint venture between NASA and the Japanese space agency NASDA. During the eight-day mission, the crew conducted 44 experiments in life sciences and materials processing. As a mission specialist, Dr. Jemison was responsible for conducting experiments on the effects of weightlessness and motion sickness on the crew.

She worked on experiments examining bone cell research and the loss of calcium in bones during spaceflight‚Äîresearch with implications for understanding osteoporosis. She also studied biofeedback techniques to control motion sickness, a common problem for astronauts. Her dual training as a physician and engineer made her uniquely qualified to conduct this medical research in the demanding space environment.

During the mission, Endeavour orbited Earth 127 times, traveling 3.3 million miles before landing at Kennedy Space Center on September 20, 1992. Dr. Jemison logged 190 hours, 30 minutes, and 23 seconds in space, working long shifts to maximize the scientific return from the mission. She performed her duties with exceptional competence while also finding moments to appreciate the profound beauty and significance of viewing Earth from space.

In interviews after the flight, Dr. Jemison spoke about the responsibility she felt as the first Black woman in space: "I felt I was representing lots of different folks‚ÄîAfrican Americans, women, all people who had not had the opportunity to do this before." She also honored her heritage by bringing several symbolic items aboard Endeavour, including a poster from the Alvin Ailey American Dance Theater and a flag from the Alpha Kappa Alpha sorority, her college sorority.

Life After NASA: Entrepreneur and Visionary

In March 1993, less than six months after her historic spaceflight, Dr. Jemison made the surprising decision to leave NASA. Rather than flying additional missions, she chose to pursue other ways of making a difference. She founded The Jemison Group, a technology consulting firm that develops science and technology solutions for everyday challenges with a focus on improving healthcare delivery in developing countries.

Dr. Jemison also established the Dorothy Jemison Foundation for Excellence (named after her mother) in 1994. The foundation's flagship program, The Earth We Share (TEWS), is an international science camp that brings together students ages 12-16 from around the world for a four-week intensive program focused on problem-solving and critical thinking. The program emphasizes that science and technology are for everyone, regardless of background, and that solving global challenges requires diverse perspectives.

In the years following her NASA career, Dr. Jemison has taught at Cornell University and Dartmouth College, bringing her unique experiences to the classroom. She founded BioSentient Corporation, which develops products based on technology that monitors and optimizes autonomic nervous system function. She has served on numerous corporate boards and has been an advocate for science education, particularly for underrepresented minorities.

100 Year Starship: Building the Future of Interstellar Travel

In 2011, Dr. Jemison took on perhaps her most ambitious project yet when she founded the 100 Year Starship (100YSS) organization. Funded initially by a grant from DARPA (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency), the mission of 100YSS is to make the capability of human travel beyond our solar system to another star a reality within the next 100 years.

The 100 Year Starship isn't focused solely on building a specific spacecraft. Instead, it's working to create an inclusive, audacious journey that transforms life on Earth while enabling interstellar travel. The organization brings together scientists, engineers, artists, educators, and visionaries to tackle the enormous challenges of interstellar flight‚Äîpropulsion systems that can achieve a significant fraction of light speed, life support for multi-generational voyages, navigation across vast distances, and countless other technical hurdles.

But Dr. Jemison insists that 100YSS is about more than technology. She believes that pursuing interstellar travel will generate innovations that improve life on Earth, from energy generation to materials science to social organization. She also emphasizes that this endeavor must be inclusive, involving people from all nations, cultures, and backgrounds. As she says, "It's not about going to the stars, it's about getting the world to think about the journey to the stars."

The 100 Year Starship holds annual public symposia where researchers present cutting-edge work on topics ranging from warp drive physics to social organization aboard generation ships. The organization awards seed grants to innovative projects and works to inspire young people to pursue the science and engineering that will make interstellar travel possible. Through 100YSS, Dr. Jemison is ensuring that humanity's reach toward the stars continues, building on the legacy of exploration she helped create with her own spaceflight.

Cultural Impact and Continuing Inspiration

Dr. Mae Jemison's impact extends far beyond her scientific and technological contributions. She has become a cultural icon, appearing on television shows including an episode of Star Trek: The Next Generation (making her the first real astronaut to appear on a Star Trek series). She has received numerous honorary doctorates and has been inducted into the National Women's Hall of Fame and the International Space Hall of Fame.

She continues to advocate passionately for science education and for increasing diversity in STEM fields. She speaks frequently about the importance of having scientists and engineers from all backgrounds, arguing that innovation requires diverse perspectives and that excluding any group of people limits our collective problem-solving capacity. She encourages young people, particularly girls and minorities, to pursue their passions in science without feeling they must choose between different interests or conform to narrow stereotypes of what scientists should be.

Dr. Jemison's life demonstrates that barriers can be broken, that childhood dreams can be achieved through dedication and excellence, and that one person can make contributions across multiple fields while inspiring millions. From the South Side of Chicago to orbital space and beyond, Mae Jemison has lived by the principle that the future doesn't have to be limited by the present, and that with courage, intelligence, and determination, we can reach for the stars‚Äîand actually touch them.

Legacy: Dancing Among the Stars

Dr. Mae Jemison's legacy is multifaceted, reflecting her refusal to be confined to a single role or identity. She is simultaneously a physician, engineer, astronaut, entrepreneur, educator, and dancer‚Äîproving that human beings need not limit themselves to narrow categories. This holistic approach to life and career has inspired countless young people, particularly women and minorities, to pursue their diverse passions without apology or compromise.

Her historic spaceflight aboard Endeavour shattered a barrier that had stood since the beginning of human space exploration. By becoming the first Black woman in space, she expanded the definition of who could be an astronaut and demonstrated that space belonged to all of humanity, not just a privileged few. Young girls of color could now see someone who looked like them floating in orbit, conducting experiments, and representing humanity's exploratory spirit.

Perhaps equally important is Dr. Jemison's post-NASA career, which demonstrates that there are many ways to contribute to human progress. Rather than remaining at NASA for additional flights, she chose to leverage her unique experience and credibility to launch ventures aimed at solving global challenges. The Jemison Group works to improve healthcare in developing countries. The Dorothy Jemison Foundation brings together young people from around the world to tackle scientific problems collaboratively. BioSentient Corporation develops technology with practical health applications.

The 100 Year Starship project embodies Dr. Jemison's visionary thinking at its finest. While critics might dismiss interstellar travel as impractical or impossible, she argues that pursuing audacious goals drives innovation with immediate practical benefits. The technologies needed for interstellar flight‚Äîadvanced propulsion, sustainable life support systems, efficient energy generation‚Äîhave applications for addressing climate change, resource scarcity, and other challenges facing Earth today. By organizing global collaboration around this grand vision, 100YSS is already generating innovations that improve life on our home planet.

Dr. Jemison's advocacy for diversity in STEM fields continues to shape how institutions think about inclusion. She doesn't argue for diversity as charity or fairness alone, though those are worthy goals. Instead, she makes the practical case that excluding any group of people limits our collective intelligence and problem-solving capacity. The challenges humanity faces‚Äîfrom climate change to disease to sustainable energy‚Äîare too complex for any homogeneous group to solve alone. We need the perspectives, creativity, and talents of all people.

When young people learn about Mae Jemison, they learn that the barriers they face are not permanent, that excellence can overcome discrimination, and that their dreams‚Äîhowever audacious‚Äîare valid and achievable. They learn that scientists can also be dancers, that doctors can become astronauts, and that a girl from the South Side of Chicago who watches the moon landings on TV can grow up to orbit Earth herself. Most importantly, they learn that the future is not predetermined‚Äîit's created by people who refuse to accept that "this is just the way it is" and who work with dedication, intelligence, and courage to make it different. That may be Dr. Mae Jemison's greatest legacy of all.

Frequently Asked Questions

What did Mae Jemison accomplish as an astronaut?
On September 12, 1992, Dr. Mae Jemison became the first African American woman to travel to space when she flew aboard Space Shuttle Endeavour mission STS-47. During the eight-day mission, she orbited Earth 127 times and conducted experiments in life sciences and material sciences, serving as a mission specialist and science mission specialist. She logged 190 hours, 30 minutes, and 23 seconds in space.
What is the 100 Year Starship project?
The 100 Year Starship is an organization founded by Mae Jemison in 2011 to ensure the capabilities for human interstellar travel exist within the next 100 years. The project works to foster innovation in science and technology needed for interstellar flight while generating transformative benefits for life on Earth. It brings together scientists, engineers, artists, and visionaries to tackle the challenges of traveling to another star system.
What education does Mae Jemison have?
Mae Jemison earned a Bachelor of Science in Chemical Engineering and a Bachelor of Arts in African and African-American Studies from Stanford University in 1977. She then earned her Doctor of Medicine degree from Cornell University Medical College in 1981, making her both an engineer and a physician. This dual expertise in engineering and medicine made her uniquely qualified for space medicine research.
What did Mae Jemison do after NASA?
After leaving NASA in 1993, Mae Jemison founded The Jemison Group technology consulting firm, established the Dorothy Jemison Foundation for Excellence, created BioSentient Corporation, and taught at Cornell and Dartmouth. She founded the 100 Year Starship project in 2011 and continues to advocate for science education and increasing diversity in STEM fields. She serves on corporate boards and speaks globally about science, technology, and human potential.
Why did Mae Jemison appear on Star Trek?
Dr. Jemison is a longtime Star Trek fan and credits Lieutenant Uhura (played by Nichelle Nichols) with inspiring her to pursue space exploration. When she became an astronaut, LeVar Burton (who played Geordi La Forge) invited her to appear on Star Trek: The Next Generation. She appeared in the 1993 episode "Second Chances," becoming the first real astronaut to appear on any Star Trek series. This appearance symbolized how science fiction inspired real achievements and how those achievements could inspire future generations.
